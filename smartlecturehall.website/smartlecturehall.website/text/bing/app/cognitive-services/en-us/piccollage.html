Looking for opportunities in the mobile app space

Cardinal Blue Software cofounders John Fan and Ching-Mei Chen started out building games and other applications on the Facebook platform. When Apple launched the iPhone and iPad, they quickly recognized a new opportunity.

 

“We saw that the market was moving to tablets and mobile devices,” says Chen, Head of Business and Partnerships at Cardinal Blue. She explains that the company was looking for a way to use its creativity and design skills to develop mobile apps that would attract and delight millions of customers. “We took what we had learned building Facebook apps, combined that with our knowledge of customer psychology, and started asking what people would be willing to pay to have on their mobile devices. We quickly narrowed it down to photos—something timeless that everyone enjoys and cares about.”

 

The result was PicCollage, an app that allows customers to display multiple photos on one screen and position them in various ways to form collages. “We launched the first version of PicCollage in the App Store in 2011, Apple featured it in the New & Noteworthy list, and within a few days of release we had tens of thousands of downloads,” Chen says. An Android version of the app soon followed and, in 2015, Cardinal Blue launched PicCollage for Windows 10.

 

That first version of PicCollage was a simple, one-screen photo-collage app. Customers could add multiple photos and move them around the screen, but they couldn’t save or share their creations. “If you wanted to share a collage, you had to take a screen shot,” Chen says.

 

Since those early days, Cardinal Blue has worked hard to improve PicCollage, making it possible for customers to add text and stickers to their photos, include videos and animated gifs in their collages, share their work on social media, and print the images onto physical objects. In 2013, the company raised US$2.3 million in seed funding from Silicon Valley investors 500 Startups, Floodgate Fund, Freestyle Capital, Quest Venture Partners, Sand Hill Angels, and XG Ventures to build out its social platform.

 

Today, Cardinal Blue is profitable and no longer has to rely on outside funding. The company has 18 employees—about half of them are developers—and offices in both San Francisco and Taipei. The PicCollage app has been downloaded more than 120 million times, has 15 million active users monthly, and continues to see steady growth. PicCollage is also a Top 10 mobile photo app for iOS and Android in the United States, United Kingdom, Japan, and many other countries.

 

An important feature for PicCollage customers is the ability to search the Internet from within the app to find photos, videos, and other images—but Cardinal Blue encountered some serious problems with the first version of its web search feature. “We were using the Google Image Search API for web search, but we started getting complaints from parents and teachers about inappropriate content in the search results,” says John Fan, Cofounder and CEO at PicCollage.

 

Exploring new tools and capabilities

After deciding to replace the Google API for web searching within PicCollage, Fan says the company researched a lot of alternatives. Cardinal Blue finally settled on the Bing Image Search API from Microsoft Cognitive Services, one of several application programming interfaces (APIs) based on expertise developed within Microsoft Research. “Our key reasons for choosing the Bing Image Search API were its flexibility and power, plus its good ‘safe search’ capability,” Fan says. “Bing also allows related searches, which we’ll be adding to our app soon.”

 

Using the Bing Image Search API has worked out well for Cardinal Blue. “Users are saying that search results in our app are now more relevant and safer than when we were using the Google Image Search API,” Chen says. ”Teachers who wrote in complaining before have circled back to offer compliments. It has been a huge plus having the Bing Image Search API in PicCollage.”

 

Cardinal Blue found that working with Microsoft to solve its search problem also led to an unexpected bonus. “When we started talking to the Bing team, they introduced us to all these other APIs and encouraged us to play around with them,” says Fan, who notes that the other APIs can analyze images, identify emotions on human faces, recognize colors, and perform many other complex functions. “It didn’t take us long to realize Microsoft Cognitive Services had handed us a powerful set of computer-vision and artificial-intelligence tools that we could use to create great apps and new features for our customers in just a few hours.”

 

Chen concurs: “When the Bing team showed us the other Cognitive Services APIs, they also provided a roadmap for the tools that they had in place, what was coming, and which APIs they thought could help us create great features within PicCollage. It was encouraging having another team excited about our app and building it with us. It felt like having a partner in our business.”

 

Experimenting with different APIs quickly led to discoveries that the company has already used to add new features to PicCollage and to build a new app called InstaCaption, which uses the Computer Vision API from Microsoft Cognitive Services to evaluate what is going on in a photo and then automatically generate a playful caption.

 

Cardinal Blue recently launched a new PicCollage feature that allows customers to use rage comics—a popular form of online visual communication, similar to memes or emoticons—to create special effects in their photos. Fan and Chen believe their new Rage Faces feature will make PicCollage more fun and may help to expand their customer base. “To create the Rage Faces effect, we are using the Emotion API from Microsoft Cognitive Services,” Fan says. “The idea is that the APIs tell us the location of the face in the photo, and the emotion associated with it. The app then chooses a comic face that matches the emotion and position of the face in the photo, and you get a comic version of that person’s face.”

 

Chen says the Microsoft Cognitive Services APIs have given Cardinal Blue a new way of looking at the mobile app market, and the company is already making plans to use the APIs to develop new products and complete future integrations with PicCollage.

 

 

Developing mobile apps easier and faster

Fan and Chen say they and their developers are impressed with how easy it is to use the Microsoft Cognitive Services APIs, how flexible the APIs are, and how much value they provide by making it simple to implement complex functions.

 

“We’ve used the Cognitive Services APIs to build many side apps during our internal hack days,” Chen says. “Our developers love hack days. They get to drop everything and just play for a day.” Chen explains that on hack days the team gets together to brainstorm, developers discuss which APIs would be fun to try out on some of their “crazy ideas” that previously would have been impossible to build in a day, and then they split into different groups and get to work. By the end of the day, the team produces a handful of creative and fun applications of the APIs.

 

“Our developers have these great tools to work with, so they can unleash their product creativity,” she says. “They don’t have to think about writing algorithms. They can focus on the functionality in the Cognitive Services APIs and incorporate it into their app ideas. Later, we can choose which of those apps we want to refine and release, but the basic work happens in a single day.”

 

According to Fan, when his team launches a new app, they like to start with one platform and gather information before moving to others, but eventually they want parity across platforms. Because Microsoft Cognitive Services provides Representational State Transfer (REST) APIs that use the HTTP protocol, the APIs perform well on all popular platforms, and developers can enable them with just a few lines of code.  

 

“The Microsoft Cognitive Services APIs are platform-agnostic,” Fan says. “We can call them from any platform and get the results we want, very quickly.”

 

The effects are impressive, says Fan, reflecting the use of machine learning and other complex data science in the APIs. “What’s interesting about the Cognitive Services APIs is that they are not just doing traditional machine learning,” he says. “They are doing deep learning, which involves much more complicated capabilities, such as sophisticated image description, recognition, and captioning. Just a few years ago, this kind of deep learning was not available anywhere. The fact that it is now available through a set of easy-to-use APIs from Microsoft is incredible—and very powerful.”

 

Unleashing creativity and gaining competitive advantage

“For our customers, the effects these Microsoft APIs create are like magic,” Fan says. “And having these magical capabilities lets them go from ordinary photos to something beautiful and sharable very quickly. We want to give our customers more than just a usable tool. We want to make them go, ‘Wow!’ These APIs help us add that wow factor.”

 

Chen says the Microsoft Cognitive Services APIs have given Cardinal Blue much more flexibility and breadth in thinking about and building new apps and features. “The APIs have opened a lot of doors for us in terms of creativity and design,” she says. “Speed is also a huge advantage in app development, and we can move much faster now. We don’t have to reinvent the wheel. We can try a lot of things quickly, moving on when something doesn’t work or doubling down when it does.”

 

“The Microsoft Cognitive Services APIs allow us to focus on our competitive advantage—our creativity,” Fan says. “By adding our creativity on top of the technology Microsoft provides, we can build products and services that surprise and delight our customers while also being useful and relevant. That’s an exciting opportunity.”
